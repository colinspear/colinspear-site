Taking big project slowdown seriously

An evaluation of one (or more) of Patrick Collison’s “Fast” projects compared to something similar today

Why might big projects take longer now than in the past?

  • Regulation both good and bad

    • health and safety
    • environment

  • other

    • more alternatives available now than in the past (even if the alternatives aren’t that great)

I feel like there’s been a major formalizing of economic, social and political life over the last century that is somehow related to this. It also reminds me of Levi-Strauss' lamentations in the first chapter of Tristes Tropiques where (in 1970 or so) he explains why, after a life spent traveling all over the world, decades before it was commonplace, he has given up the venture.

Now that the Polynesian islands have been smothered in concrete and turned into aircraft carriers solidly anchored in the southern seas, when the whole of Asia is beginning to look like a dingy suburb, when shanty-towns are spreading across Africa, when civil and military aircraft blight the primeval innocence of the American or Melanesian forests even before destroying their virginity, what else can the socalled escapism of travelling do than confront us with the more unfortunate aspects of our history? Our great Western civilization, which has created the marvels we now enjoy, has only succeeded in producing them at the cost of corresponding ills. The order and harmony of the Western world, its most famous achievement, and a laboratory in which structures of a complexity as yet unknown are being fashioned, demand the elimination of a prodigious mass of noxious by-products which now contaminate the globe. The first thing we see as we travel round the world is our own filth, thrown into the face of mankind.

This is supported by communication technologies increasing the speed and lowering the barriers to entry of information flows. This allows for both formal rules to be more easily followed and enforced (think TurboTax or paying parking tickets online, police records being fully searchable, AI video surveillance, etc, etc) and for cultural norms to evolve more rapidly and converge more quickly.

This world where it is increasingly easy to follow and enforce the rules makes it much more difficult for a passionate visionary with a bit of support to brute force her vision into action. Handshakes are worth less and greased palms are soaped and scrubbed and gaining the favor of one person doesnt generally carry the weight that it used to (this is even more diluted as bureaucracies grow in size and complexity so approvals require broader support and have to travel through more layers of process).

This all sounds bad, but it is all part of the same process that creates stable institutions and has propelled much of the growth in the last decades that has lifted so many people out of poverty.
