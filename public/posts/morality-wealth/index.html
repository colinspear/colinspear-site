Is it immoral to be rich?

2021-05-27
posts , drafts , wealth , philosophy

Motivation

The idea was something I was exposed to and sort of absorbed growing up. I remember having a conversation with some friends who clearly held this view. We were talking about Bill Gates and they seemed to be convinced that he was an evil person which just didn’t square with my ideas about the economic, intellectual and technological advances from home computing (not to mention the philanthropy of the Gates Foundation).

I am curious about the history and psychology of this idea.

Notes

Arguments that wealth is unethical

Philosophical arguments

From Stanford Encyclopedia:

  • For instance, the full Biblical quotation says that “the love of money is the root of all [kinds of] evil” (1 Timothy 6:10). To have a “love of money”, or (in less moralistic words) a profit motive, means to seek money for its own sake.
  • Three main strands of critique:
    1. Money is not a real thing, but just a means to an end, a tool to make the necessary exchange of good easier, grease in the cogs so to speak:

    there is something unnatural about the profit motive itself. For example, Aristotle argued that we should treat objects in ways that are befitting to their fundamental nature, and since money is not meant to be a good in itself but only a medium of exchange (see section 1.1), he concluded that it is unnatural to desire money as an end in itself (Politics, 1252a–1260b).

    1. The pursuit of money = greed. This is close in nature to the argument that it is unethical to be rich while others are poor.

    A second variation of the criticism concerns the character, or more precisely the vice, that the profit motive is thought to exemplify (see also virtue ethics). To have a love for money is typically associated with selfishness and greed, i.e., a desire to have as much as possible for oneself and/or more than one really needs (McCarty 1988, Walsh & Lynch 2008).

    1. The desire to be rich is in opposition to other, more upstanding desires. If you want to be rich, you can’t also want to be good:

    the profit motive signals the absence of more appropriate motives. Kant argued that actions only have moral worth if they are performed for moral reasons, or, more specifically, for the sake of duty.

Usury, or making money on money (interest)

This seems to be where money losses most of its merit points (at least from a historical perspective). I also happen to believe these are also the meatiest arguments.

Popular arguments

  • People don’t get rich through merit or hard work, but rather through a corrupt, inequitable system
  • Regardless of how your wealth is obtained, when poverty and deprivation exist in the world, holding money is wrong.

Arguments that wealth ain’t so bad

From Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

  1. Adam Smith’s defense that self interested behavior actually promoted the public good by providing good and services that folks are happy to purchase in a few market. This is supported by the idea that if I trade something I have for something I want, after I make the exchange I’m better off than I was beforehand. I must value the thing I got more than the thing I had.

This argument is typically viewed as a consequentialist vindication of the profit motive (see also consequentialism): positive societal effects can morally outweigh the possible shortcomings in individual virtue (Flew 1976).

  1. Pursuing profits actually ain’t that bad!

the profit motive can exemplify a positive virtue. For example, there is the well-known Protestant work ethic that emphasizes the positive nature of hard work, discipline and frugality (Long 1972, Wesley 1771).
