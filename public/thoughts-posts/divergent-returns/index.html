Inspired by an exchange between Rob Henderson and David Perell about the importance of “being able to get rich” as an incentive to draw overeducated people into the internet content production machine1 economy.

Links

  • unbuilders

Motivating questions

  • What are some high value add activities / businesses / sectors where economic returns don’t seem to match social benefits?
  • Where is the economic return to business far lower than the social benefit?
  • What are the career paths (traditional or otherwise) for highly educated, savvy, disciplined individuals outside of the academy?
  • How do we think about the social costs and benefits of the internet creator economy?

Unbuilders is a business in Vancouver that deconstructs houses that would otherwise be torn down to salvage with a really creative business model. They charge for deconstruction costs, then donate the reclaimed materials to Habitat for Humanity for resale. What would be a substantially more expensive process becomes substantially less expensive than simply tearing the structure down and hauling the remains to the landfill due to federal and provincial tax credits. I initially though that the donation to Habitat for Humanity was key to obtain the tax credits, but their website doesn’t actually specify whether this is true or not. I don’t know if they are leveraging multiple tax credits for the donation and for diverting waste from the landfill or what.

Everybody loves restaurants but they are notoriously impossible to make money on, either as an owner or an employee and have very challenging work environments.

  1. This sounds a bit more Orwellian than I intended, but there is enough truth in it that I think is important that I am going to leave it. I believe this particular truth comes from the answer to questions like: How much value does the “creator economy” actually add? Is net social value positive or negative? What would be the counterfactual career path for Physics PhDs (for example) who go into YouTube content creation instead of a life in the academy? Are these people drawn away from what would be otherwise meaningful careers in nonprofit management, entrepreneurship, politics, etc? I don’t know, but I’m not overly optimistic about it and fear that the best case scenario is mind tingling infotainment. ↩︎
